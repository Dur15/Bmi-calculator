BMI Guide

What is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a value derived from mass (weight) and height. It's used to categorize weight ranges that may lead to health problems.

  • Underweight: BMI < 18.5
  • Normal weight: 18.5–24.9
  • Overweight: 25–29.9
  • Obese: BMI ≥ 30

How to Calculate

The formula is: BMI = weight (kg) / (height (m))² For imperial units: BMI = [weight (lbs) / (height (in))²] × 703

Health Implications

BMI correlates with body fat and health risks:

  • High BMI → Increased risk of heart disease, diabetes
  • Low BMI → Nutritional deficiencies, osteoporosis

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is BMI for measuring health?

BMI is a useful population-level screening tool but has limitations for individuals. It doesn't distinguish between muscle and fat, and may be less accurate for:

  • Athletes with high muscle mass
  • Pregnant women
  • Elderly individuals with muscle loss
  • Certain ethnic groups with different body compositions

Should children use the same BMI categories?

No, pediatric BMI uses age and sex-specific percentiles. For children 2-19 years old:

  • Underweight: BMI < 5th percentile
  • Healthy weight: 5th to <85th percentile
  • Overweight: 85th to <95th percentile
  • Obese: ≥95th percentile

What are better alternatives to BMI?

For more precise health assessments, consider:

  • Waist circumference: Measures abdominal fat
  • Body fat percentage: Via skinfold calipers or DEXA scans
  • Waist-to-hip ratio: Better predictor of cardiovascular risk
  • Blood tests: Cholesterol, blood sugar, etc.

Why does BMI matter for health?

BMI correlates with several health risks:

  • High BMI: Increased risk of type 2 diabetes, hypertension, heart disease
  • Low BMI: Higher risk of osteoporosis, anemia, immune issues

However, BMI is just one indicator among many for health assessment.
